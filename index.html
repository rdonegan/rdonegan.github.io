ZWPA Waste Audit App |
Spring 2015

Designed for non-profit, Zero Waste Pennsylvania (ZWPA), the ZWPA App aims to improve the waste auditing process by reducing reliance on paper records and streamlining content gathering

...

ZWPA is a non-profit seeking to reduce waste generated by organizations throughout Pennsylvania. As part of its initiatives, ZWPA performs waste audits in order to identify how trash, recycling, and compost can be better disposed of within an organization’s waste infrastructure. Initially, all of ZWPA's records were handwritten and recorded on a series of paper documents later used to generate computerized reports. No formal content organization existed, and ZWPA lacked a way to archive and revisit past client requests.

As design lead on a team of four students, I drove efforts to first understand ZWPA’s current process as well as identify how design could be employed to help ZWPA better achieve its goals. Initially, my team started by creating a workflow and affinity diagram based on ZWPA interviews and observing their workspace.


Based on our research, our team chose to address several key issues:

  • Eliminating ZWPA’s reliance on paper documents.
  • Creating an efficient and reliable interface for archiving and documenting audits.
  • Taking advantage of ZWPA’s access to tablet technology.

Thus, my team worked to design and implement a web application that would digitize the audit process. With our application, ZWPA members are able to more efficiently complete audits in the field, as well as access important data from any internet-enabled device be it mobile, tablet, or desktop.

FeedMe |
Fall 2014

FeedMe utilizes a common trend in social media - taking and sharing pictures of food - to enhance the process of locating new meals and dishes

...

Simply, FeedMe allows users to input their craving of choice and browse how others have recently enjoyed similar cravings. Users’ preferences are added to a customizable menu from which new foods or dishes can be added and removed per the preference of the specific user. With a few simple queries, hungry clients can be motivated to create inspired works of culinary art.

Our goal with this project was to construct a simplified, single-page experience to satisfy a client need. We utilized the Instagram API to draw on recently shared images - ensuring different results and experiences with each new query. To reduce any learning curves, we consolidated all interactions within a single side-bar from which all critical tasks can be performed. From this control center, users can enter new queries, review recommended search terms (for inspiration), and control their menu.

Ideally, FeedMe would allow additional functionality in the way of recipes and restaurant reviews. For our initial MVP release, however, we limited the functionality of image queries to simply allow redirection to the original Instagram page.

Campus Scheduling |
Spring 2014

For my first encounter with contextual inquiry, I collaborated on research involving Administrative Assistants at Carnegie Mellon University

...

Tasked with proposing improvements to the current system of scheduling external speakers and guests, my team began by performing observations. Our goal was to understand the AA’s process of scheduling visitors on campus. Our observations included related tasks such as reimbursement and managing travel arrangements.

Based on our observations, we interpreted our notes into flow and sequence models. We recorded the various roles involved in the scheduling process, as well as noted any breakdowns in the current system. This allowed us to identify the primary players in a typical flow (the AA and visitor) as well as auxiliary interactors (reimbursement department, travel agency, hotel, catering company, AA supervisor).



To get a better sense of how the responsibilities of these various groups interacted, we then focused our attention on consolidating our notes - observing where there was overlap and where users had similar feedback. We compiled our findings in an affinity diagram and consolidated flow model.


Our findings allowed us to identify the key pain points throughout visitor scheduling. We divided these primary procedural breakdowns into two groups: “Pre-Visit” and “Post-Visit”. We found that most of the work done by AA’s occurred within these two phases, and that a successful “Pre-Visit” often resulted in an easier “Post-Visit”. Based on this, we identified three primary goals for our process redesign:

  • Re-evaluate communication system between AA and visitor.
  • Address difficulties in extracting visitor information for the AA.
  • Facilitate use of information & data in the future (i.e. in the post-visit).
    •

Our group iterated through a variety of solutions, using modeling and sketching to brainstorm. We tried to target one breakdown in particular in order to design an elegant, robust solution making use of existing processes and technology. We settled on a design we felt would be most effective in facilitating the pre-visit process as well as future data retrieval.

Our final solution took advantage of the email correspondence already utilized by AA’s when communicating with prospective visitors. In its proposed form, our system would allow for the AA to send one, unified form to a designated recipient for data gathering purposes.

The form would allow for entry of all standard information requested by AA’s before a visit (travel arrangements, date of arrival, guests, etc.) while avoiding the back-and-forth commonly observed in order to retrieve such information. Collected data would then be stored in a database, organized by visitor.

Making use of an accessible database would nullify some of the issues involved in organizing and retrieving information - particularly paper records from past dates. AA’s would be able to easily gather and review information to use in future processes, as well as update incorrect / inconsistent data per request of the visitor.

We presented our complete findings and vision to other students as well as campus Administrative Assistants. Our vision we represented by sketch:



We determined that key impediments to our proposal were cost & time, security, and user training & education. If allowed more time, future iterations of our revitalized system would have had to address these components.

Miller Gallery |
Fall 2013

I designed the Miller Gallery promotional website as a course project meant to advertise an Andy Warhol exhibit taking place on campus

...

Working within the content and constraints I was assigned, I was challenged with how to present the given information in a logical layout. I also hoped to incorporate some of Warhol’s artistic flair.



To best market the exhibit, it was important that I chose an architecture that wouldn’t detract from the main objective of the site. Thus, I designed a simplified navigation (“The Artist”, “The Exhibit”, “The Event”).

In my designs, I also experimented with more ‘Warholian’ colors and layout, yet I ultimately chose a more grounded approach. Here, color is used sparingly to identify areas of attention and interactivity. This I hoped would bring the content to the forefront, while also eliciting some of Warhol’s trademark style.


Architecture Lecture Series |
Fall 2013

I designed signage to promote lecture events for the Carnegie Mellon School of Architecture, as well as to demonstrate how the use of text and typefaces influence typographic hierarchy in a composition

...

My first iterations experimented with how font size, typographic weight, and other graphical elements affected my composition. From this phase, I obtained a sense of how different visual factors could affect a viewer's perception of the given material.

In my first attempt at unifying design and content, I chose what I believed to be a minimalist layout. I designed the school name as the clear point of entry, followed by a progressive listing of subsequent events. I used color sparingly to differentiate each information block, hopefully enabling quick viewing.

I experimented with a background color in my next iteration - the theory being that my previous design might not have been easily differentiated when placed beside other posters. In doing so, I felt I sacrificed too much in the way of information clarity. Additionally, I received feedback that line I had used to direct attention throughout the poster had become too dominant.

For my final design I focused on distinguishing content from the negative space. Thus, I slightly increased the font size of the event headings, and decreased the stroke of the white, directional line. I also added a complementary blue fill to diversify the background intensity, while also creating the illusion of a skyline.
