Item Selection

CHALLENGE

Designing for big data is hard, especially when it comes to scaling for the sake of usability. One of the most prevalent pain points I’ve observed in my work and in other applications is that of item selection. Here I take a stab at identifying what makes this problem so hard to design for, and what characteristics may improve this experience in other applications.

I collaborated with a project manager during this process to help draw on data. Later, I worked with another designer on refining visuals. This piece describes the high-level process and general observations of selector interactions. I have excluded high-fidelity mockups from this piece.

PROCESS

We started by analyzing the communications we’d received over a two-year period and following up with reporters as necessary. The theme of the support tickets was clear: “I can’t find what I want.”

This warranted an exploration into how selectable things - we’ll call them “items” - were created, selected and used in the software. We generated a customer journey map as an artifact of the interviews we held with client users regarding this process.

In addition, we worked with several client users and representatives to map the pain points of existing item selectors in the software. This heuristic analysis of sorts was derived from observations in which we tasked users with walking through common workflows.

LEARNING FROM DATA

To supplement our anecdotal research with something quantitative, we looked to data logs in Splunk while the goal of answering the following:

  • How many items do users select at a time?
  • Are there similarities in the types of items being selected?
  • How long does it take for users to make a selection?

On average, selection was taking 15-20 seconds with some extreme outliers. We discovered that in most cases, users re-selected the same items or items they'd just created. We sketched some quick sort concepts to put in front of users.

CONCEPT TESTING

We quickly got our sketches in front of users to evaluate their response. What we heard from nearly all of them was a lack of confidence. Users wanted a clue that what they were selecting was correct, not just the recommendations of a sort. We began integrating meta-data into our designs, playing with how we could use space and information to support a selection.

METRICS TO MOVE

Based on our research, we identified several metrics to tackle:

  • Efficiency: getting users to the thing they need, faster.  If I got this right, hopefully users wouldn’t be tempted to search at all.
  • Maintaining context: selection, like any other input, should feel a part of a larger workflow.  I aimed to avoid removing users from their workflow.
  • Transparency: with big data comes even more meta-data.  I hoped to use this information intentionally to support decision-making.

TESTING SORT AND META DATA

I took a first pass at designing low-fidelity concepts for item selection. I used a prior project as inspiration and designed a selector that would allow users to more quickly select a franchise region. Regions could easily number in the hundreds, and were likely to be given ambiguous names. For simplicity, I focused on single selection.

I held cognitive walkthroughs with users familiar with this type of paradigm. I found that users were reassured by additional meta data. Users reiterated the ambiguity of selecting items from large data sets, and appreciated the extra confirmation.

MORE ITERATIONS

I worked on another iteration - one less dramatic in its deemphasis of search though still focused on support via data.

I realized I’d also neglected two unique use cases I’d previously identified: users who want to select quickly and move on, and those who require additional support. I experimented with a two-pronged selection that literally broke these use cases into separate, albeit linked, UI’s.

I was really testing concepts here, but I played a bit with visual design - doing away with pagination and specifying interactions for item selection and hover.

RESULT

Cognitive walkthroughs with these two prototypes revealed many pain points improved. However, testing any element like this is flawed without real data. I designed an agile, progressive release plan for this kind of element in a system, using data logging to validation enhancements. I also outlined key features for logging:

  • Time spent selecting an item(s)
  • Number of search queries
  • Ranking of selected item when the user first opens the selector
  • How far a user scrolls during a session
  • Reopenings of a selector (making edits/modifications)

In addition to contributing to an agile rollout, I designed and tested vision mockups incorporating the quick-select and verbose-select I'd designed as concepts. I plan to iterate and test with these prototypes as more is learned from rollout.
