Title Closing Automations

OVERVIEW

Context. Qualia provides a platform for Settlement Agencies to conduct and track home closings.

Problem. Settlement agents lose time performing repetitive tasks during each closing.

Constraints. A patchwork of automation-like functionality existed prior to my work. Our approach had to take the pre-existing framework into account.

My Role. I was the sole designer, and worked with a product manager to research customers and design a solution.

Result. A system for Settlement Agents to automate parts of a home closing by defining triggers and actions.

Solution

Our solution built upon a framework for automating the home closing process.

  1. We expanded Qualia’s automation framework by allowing clients to define event-based triggers and actions. This reduced manual tasks per closing and increased overall output.
  2. We provided agencies the ability to define automations by state, branch, and transaction type.
  3. We built a transparent interface to allow order processors to feel confident that the actions they expected were actually taking place.

Automation Groups

To support the complexity of our user base, clients have the flexibility to apply automations universally or to specific states, order types, and branches.

Selecting Automations

We prioritized automating the most common, repetitive actions performed by customers, and allowed customers to string many automations together with a single trigger.

Filling the Gaps

For cases where an automation may not exist, clients dynamically assign tasks to order processors - all from within the same trigger.

Transparency for Non-Admin Roles

To give order processors the confidence that automations are being fulfilled correctly, we built a tool to track automations in real time as they’re applied to a closing.

THE PROBLEM

Feedback from customers and industry stakeholders revealed a few things:

  1. Industry-wide, settlements agents perform similar, repetitive tasks to complete closings
  2. Many agencies hire Processors who spend the majority of their time creating, entering, and maintaining the same information across tasks
  3. Settlement agencies already used a number of ad-hoc tools to save time

We found that our clients were losing time and efficiency due to repetitive actions across home closings. Many of these actions, we hypothesized, could be easily automated.

METHOD

We began by talking with our customers about the ways they process tasks, and tested out a few automation prototypes. Based on our early conversation, we gleaned a clearer picture of how to approach this problem in the product.

DISCOVERY

We spoke to a pool of clients varying in business size and region. Our principal goals were:

  1. Observe how our clients reason about tasks and automations
  2. Identify where clients expect to find automation-type settings in the context of our product

Conversations involved a Q&A followed by a click-through of a mid-fidelity prototype. Some highlights of our findings:

  • Mental Modal. Clients think of automations in terms of “triggers” and “actions”
  • Automations vs. Settings. Clients consistently distinguished between triggered actions and settings that are applied automatically
  • Existing Workflows. Many clients had already established complex workflows and triggers.
  • Legacy Behavior. Our product already supported automatically assigning tasks. Research revealed that clients considered this a form of automation.

THE FLOW

A few key components to the automations flow:

  1. Users start by selecting an automation trigger. Based on usage data and research, the triggers are ordered by popularity.
  2. Once a trigger is selected, users receive suggestions for common tasks and ordered by relevance.
  3. [New] Users can customize each automation depending on the automation and the result they’d like to see. For example, when automating a message be sent, users can specify the recipients and subject, and have the option to use a message template.
  4. [New] Users can add multiple automations to a single trigger.

We built upon an existing framework for creating triggers and automatically assigning tasks. Because clients had already invested significant time identifying triggers and automating task assignments, we decided to work incrementally instead of starting from scratch.

AUTOMATION MANAGEMENT

A major design focus was making it easier for admin users to find an existing automation group. Most visitors to the page would need to edit existing automations rather than create new ones.

Critically, I worked on an entirely new piece of the UI that would allow Order Processors to track automations as they were being applied. Unlike the bulk of this project, this interface would be present within each order.

PRODUCT TOUR

Finally, the feature underwent a minor rebrand: from “Smart Tasks” to “Smart Actions”.To complement the release, I designed a simple first-time experience tour.

EVIDENCE-BASED IMPROVEMENTS

Resolving Automation Errors

We noted an unusually high number of failures due to certain popular automations. Nearly 50% of certain popular automations encountered some kind of failure, thus undermining adoption. We worked on addressing the logic causing failures, and added support for new requested automations.

Automation Templates

Early usage data showed that many clients weren’t taking full advantage of our new automations. One concept I explored to address this was Automation Templates. I hypothesized that templates would help users understand the full functionality of automations more quickly without maneuvering through the logistics.

Rethinking the Framework

Qualitative feedback post-MVP suggested that longtime users were more comfortable with automations than novice users. To this effect, I explored new ways to configure automations, including a more visual approach. I hypothesized that a more visual, fluid approach to setting up triggers and automations would make the feature more approachable for first-time users, and subsequently support the more advanced branching logic of power users.
