Optimizing Decline Experiences

OVERVIEW

Problem. People who don’t meet eligibility criteria for personal loan at Prosper may have a shot somewhere else. How do we expose those options, and encourage users to learn more?

Constraints. All terms and conditions need to be clear and visible (more on that below).

My role. I worked with one other designer to share research, UX, and UI responsibilities.

Result. Our revamped concept saw a conversion increase from 25% to 65%, with more people choosing to explore their options with our partner.

THE PROBLEM

Prosper offers fixed term, low interest personal loans between $2,000 - $40,000. Of course, not everyone qualifies for a loan with Prosper, but they might have more options!

When I was brought on to the project, Prosper offered an alternative loan solution through its partner, AmOne. Conversion from Prosper to the AmOne interface hovered around 25%, while similar competitors implemented solutions averaging ~60% conversion. Clearly, there was work to be done.

USER RESEARCH

We ran a user test with five participants who met demographics similar to Prosper customers. Based on our research, we diagnosed the following:

  • People are unsurprisingly not happy to discover they’ve been declined for a loan through Prosper.
  • People don’t want to repeat themselves. They need money, and they need it fast. The prospect of reentering their loan application was not appealing.
  • The AmOne branding was not working. Evidently, the old design’s attempt to blend AmOne with Prosper styling was translating to a confusing experience.

THE IMPORTANCE OF TRANSPARENCY

It’s important to note that a goal of the project was to build customers' trust through transparency. We recognized the risk of introducing a page like this and impact it may have on customers who find it unsolicited. We needed to communicate that Prosper would only use an applicant’s information to expedite their application, and would not otherwise sell that info to third parties.

That goal manifested in a strict requirement that the AmOne disclosure needed to appear above the CTA in a legible, clear form. Design-wise, we could play with how this information appeared, but it was important that our customers understand this step before moving forward.

USER TESTING CONCEPTS

We tested rapidly across a diverse user base matching the Prosper demo. To more realistically simulate the scenario we were designing, we asked all participants to click through a fake loan application, culminating in a test version of the “AmOne page”.

THE SOLUTION

Some of the key considerations we made in our final solution:

  • Copy is key: we found that by tweaking the copy on the page, we could better acclimate users to a page none would ever have expected. By reframing as an opportunity rather than an endpoint, we tried to reframe the page itself.
  • Continuity: early research suggested that attempt to bring in external branding was jarring, and only amplified the confusion most people felt on the page. To correct this, we used only Prosper-themed components, and made the tile look like the next step in the process.
  • Simplifying the disclosure: Working with legal copy is hard, but necessary. Our final solution - an iframe - may not be the most elegant solution, but it dramatically improved transparency and was something we could optimize later.

RESULT

The final implementation of the page exceeded are expectations, with click-through on the page hitting 65%, up from a 25% average. In other words, over half of eligible applicants pursued their options with AmOne after being declined by Prosper.
